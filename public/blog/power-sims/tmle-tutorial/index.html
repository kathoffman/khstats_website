TLDR;

  • The most basic form of Targeted Maximum Likelihood Estimation (TMLE) allows you to calculate the average effect of an exposure of interest using estimates of:

This article is solely meant to help someone understand TMLE on a conceptual or applied level, and then implement it in R for a statistical analysis. Before I give a motivation for TMLE, a quick refresher on causal inference and propensity scores:

Causal inference refers to the many tools available to us to help answer questions like ‚Äúwhat would have happened if we had done this instead of that?‚Äù To truly answer these questions, we‚Äôd need to rewind time to see what would have happened if we‚Äôd done something differently. In a world without Time-Turners, our next best option is to set up an experiment where individuals randomly get this exposure, or that exposure, and then look at the differences in what happened to the two groups. In industry research, that‚Äôs called A/B testing, and in medical research, it‚Äôs a randomized control trial.

Due to practicality and ethics, this sort of experiment is not always possible. Sometimes our only option is to use the data we already have, which records what happened under various exposures that were not randomized. As statisticians, we know there‚Äôs a lot of bias in observational data like this, because individuals received (or even chose) their exposure for a myriad of reasons. We try to control for the factors that affected an individual‚Äôs exposure and outcome by adjusting for confounders in our regression model. The issue with a model like linear regression is that it is Insert Issues Here.

Propensity scores are one of the first ways statisticians came up with to help answer the this vs. that question using observational data. The idea of propensity scores is to use an estimator ‚Äì often as simple as a logistic regression model ‚Äì to get a probability estimate, or ‚Äúscore,‚Äù which represents an individual‚Äôs likelihood of receiving an exposure given their baseline covariates. We then use that score in some way to try to reduce bias in our calculation of the exposure effect.

In medicine, the intuitive understanding of propensity scores is that we want to make the estimates from our observational data closer to what they would be if they came from data on a perfectly executed randomized clinical trial.

A few common ways of using a propensity score, or probability estimate, that you may have heard of before:

  1. Matching: run your analysis on a subgroup of individuals in which every person who received the exposure has a ‚Äúmatching‚Äù person with a similar propensity score, but who did not receive the exposure. In a simple example of matching, if you have 500 people who received the exposure and 1000 who did not, you will find the 500 people who did not receive the exposure but who had propensity scores based on their baseline covariates who were closest to those that did, and then run your analysis on that subgroup of the population.

  2. Inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW): uses the propensity score to down-weight patients who did receive the exposure, or treatment, and had a high probability of receiving the exposure and upweighting patients who did not receive the exposure but had a high probability of receiving the exposure given their baseline covariates.

If we think a bit about the first option, matching, it should become obvious that by taking a subgroup of our population in this way, we are making our estimates of the exposure effect extremely prone to bias. IPTW is usually the better option of the two, and if you‚Äôd like to read a good blog post on understanding IPTW better with examples in R, I recommend Lucy Mcgowan‚Äôs blog post ‚ÄúUnderstanding Propensity Score Weighting.‚Äù

However, there‚Äôs actually another four letter acronym out there that far fewer statisticians have heard about: TMLE

TMLE aims to solve the same issue of imbalanced exposures as other propensity score methods. The most basic form of TMLE is analogous to the previous examples I gave for matching and IPTW: we have an exposure and outcome that is affected by confounders, and we‚Äôre trying to estimate the average difference in outcome if we were to switch the exposure from this to that for all individuals.

This basic form of the TMLE algorithm uses two estimates:

  1. the probability an individual receives an exposure of interest, given their baseline confounders (their propensity score)

  2. the probability an individual experiences an outcome of interest, given their exposure and baseline confounders

and uses those probability estimates to estimate the average difference in outcomes if everyone were to have received the exposure, compared to if everyone were to not have received the exposure.

The actual algorithm to estimate this probability is quite complex, but is similar to the iterative Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in maximum likelihood estimation. The wonderful news is that you don‚Äôt need to know the algorithm as an applied statistician who wants to implement TMLE! However, it‚Äôs very interesting, and if you‚Äôd like to understand more, you should read the aforementioned references.

So, why do I think TMLE is better than IPTW or other propensity score matching methods? In short, because you don‚Äôt need to fit any certain type of regression to get those probability estimates. You can use any estimator you want ‚Äì a generalized linear model, splines, random forests, gradient boosting, neural nets, honestly anything ‚Äì to get those probability estimates. You can actually use all of those estimators to get the two probability estimates, if you want! More on that later.

The benefit of expanding your toolbox of potential estimators is that most estimators are built with prediction in mind, and thus yield very good probability estimates to initiate our TMLE algorithm. When our goal as statisticians is to calculate effects on an outcome attributable to an exposure, it‚Äôs easy to shy away from these orediction-focused types of estimators, because most of them do not have any statistical theory to allow us to calculate valid confidence intervals.

The huge selling point of TMLE is that it allows you to utilize prediction-focused, often non-parametric, estimators, but still obtain valid confidence intervals on your final estimate of the exposure‚Äôs effect. In other methods, like IPTW, we can only obtain valid confidence intervals if we obtain our propensity score using pre-specified parametric models. For example, we fit a logistic regression for our exposure predicted by eight pre-specified baseline covariates.

This example logistic model to calculate the propensity score is not only placing a strong distributional assumption on our data, but it is limited in its ability to take in high-, or even medium-dimensional data. When we use estimators that are well equipped for very ‚Äúwide‚Äù data and perform variable selection, we obtain better overall estimates for our exposure effect of interest.

There is another benefit to TMLE: it has very good bias-variance properties, and those properties are ‚Äúrobust,‚Äù or ‚Äúresistant to‚Äù model mispecification, or having the wrong type of estimator and/or the wrong variables in your estimation of either the exposure or outcome.
